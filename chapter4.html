Chapter IV

The Path Rekindled

Kairo hurried down the street, chasing the fading sunlight before it disappeared behind the buildings. The final words of the ancient tree echoed in his mind like a distant drumbeat. Three years. Five Doctrines. A destiny older than the world itself. Fear pressed against his chest. So did anger. So did doubt. Maybe all of them were the same thing now.

The sky burned orange as the sun dipped toward the horizon. People passed him on the sidewalk, voices blending into meaningless noise. His thoughts were louder than the world around him. Then suddenly—Crash.

Someone slammed into him. A laptop slipped from the stranger’s hands and struck the pavement with a dull thud. “Ah—sorry! Sorry, I wasn’t watching—” the boy said quickly, crouching to grab the laptop. Kairo froze. That voice. He knew it.

The boy stood up. Their eyes met. Kairo’s breath caught. “…Korosu?”

The boy blinked, stunned. “K–Kairo?” he stammered. “No way… you’re alive? You’re actually—” He stopped himself, staring. “I thought you moved away,” Korosu said quietly. “After your parents… I never saw you again.”

Memories flooded back instantly. The narrow alley beside their houses. Bicycles racing across cracked pavement. Late afternoons sharing snacks and talking about nothing. Korosu—the neighbor who knocked on his door almost every day. The only friend Kairo had before everything fell apart. Kairo felt something heavy settle in his chest. “I didn’t move,” he said slowly.

Korosu frowned. “Then why did you disappear?”

Kairo hesitated. “I… didn’t want anyone to find me.”

Korosu looked at him for a long moment. “I checked your house every day for months,” he said quietly. “Then one day the windows were shut. No lights. No sign of you.”

The guilt hit harder than Kairo expected. He had vanished without a word. To everyone else, it must have looked like he simply abandoned his life. “I’m sorry,” Kairo muttered. “I should’ve said something.”

Korosu studied his face carefully. “Are you okay?” he asked softly. The question cracked something open inside Kairo. He exhaled slowly. “…Not really.”

Korosu didn’t push. He simply waited. So Kairo told him everything. The void. The locket. The hooded figure. The Scythe of Ashen Light. The Five Doctrines. Velran. The prophecy. Three years until the world ends. Korosu listened without interrupting. He didn’t laugh. He didn’t call Kairo insane. But every time the story darkened, his jaw tightened slightly. When Kairo finished, silence hung between them.

Korosu closed his laptop slowly. “Alright,” he said. “I’ll help.”

“You… believe me?” Kairo asked.

Korosu shrugged. “You never lied to me when we were kids,” he said. “And whatever you’re dealing with right now… you’re definitely not making it up.” Korosu invited kairo to stay at his apartment. For the first time since the locket awakened, the pressure on Kairo’s chest eased slightly. Not gone. But lighter. And that was enough.

The next two weeks became a different kind of war. Not with monsters. Not with blades. With information. Every night they worked in Korosu’s small apartment, surrounded by books, glowing monitors, and piles of scribbled notes. Korosu searched digital archives. Kairo dug through ancient mythology and obscure historical texts. Sometimes they thought they were close. But every lead collapsed.

One night they believed they had found Velran’s research logs hidden inside an abandoned university database. It took Korosu six hours to break through the encryption. When the files finally opened—they contained nothing but outdated climate reports. Another time they chased a reference to the “Doctrine of Ashen Light” through several ancient manuscripts. It ended with a medieval priest describing a farming ritual. Dead end after dead end. Still, they kept searching. Because if Velran left clues behind—they had to exist somewhere.

Then on the twelfth night, something strange happened. Korosu was digging through an abandoned academic directory buried deep in the internet. Most of the files were corrupted or empty. Then suddenly—a webpage flickered onto the screen. Not a normal page. Something… broken. Symbols filled the screen. Lines. Numbers. Strange markings twisting together like fragments of a language that didn’t belong to any civilization they knew.

Korosu leaned closer. “This part looks like math,” he said slowly. “Or measurements.”

Kairo stared at the screen. “I’ve never seen symbols like that.”

Korosu copied the strange sequence into a notepad. $\Delta-51.1657^\circ / \Theta-10.4515^\circ E / \psi-004.991$ They both stared at it. To them, it looked like corrupted code.

Korosu scratched his head. “It’s weird,” he said. “The symbols are mathematical, but the structure is wrong. Whoever wrote this wasn’t using standard coordinate formatting.”

“So it’s useless?” Kairo asked.

“Maybe not,” Korosu said thoughtfully. He leaned forward again. “It could be encryption.”

Kairo frowned. “Encryption?”

“Yeah. If Velran created his own encoding system, he could hide real coordinates inside mathematical symbols. Anyone else would assume it’s broken data.”

Kairo stared at the numbers again. “So it might actually mean something.”

“Maybe,” Korosu said. “But until we figure out the key, it’s just… noise.”

Still, they printed the page. Korosu pinned the sheet onto the corkboard above his desk beside old notes, maps, and abandoned theories. “There,” Korosu said. “We’ll crack it later.”

Kairo nodded slowly. The symbols still felt… heavy somehow. Like they were hiding something important. But exhaustion drowned the thought. They shut down the laptop. As usual, Korosu told his dad he was staying over. The two of them collapsed into sleep. Neither of them realized—they had just uncovered the first real clue Velran left behind. And somewhere beyond the city—something else had noticed.
