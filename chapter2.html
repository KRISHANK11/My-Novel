Chapter III

The Last Seed

Kairo awoke to the sound of distant traffic and the scent of dust hanging in the air. His eyes opened slowly. The cracked ceiling above him was all too familiar. The walls were plain and unchanged. Somehow, impossibly, he was back in his room. Earth.

He sat up, drenched in sweat, his breath shallow and uneven. His heart pounded like a warning bell inside his chest. Instinctively, his hand reached for the cold metal resting against him. The locket. Still there. Still real. He stared at it for a long moment, then stood abruptly. Rage and confusion surged through him.

With a shout, he tore the locket from his neck and hurled it across the room. “I don’t understand any of this!” he shouted, his voice shaking. “They say I’m the ‘chosen one’? What does that even mean?!”

The locket clattered to the floor and lay still. Silent. Unyielding. Anger. Confusion. Helplessness.

He couldn’t stay here. He needed air. Throwing on his jacket, Kairo left the house and walked without direction. The city blurred around him—its noise, its light, its indifference. Eventually, his feet led him to a place he had not visited in years.

An old park. Weathered. Faded. At its heart stood a massive, ancient tree. Its bark was rough and cracked, its branches spread wide like arms holding the sky. He had come here often as a child. It was a place of quiet and memory. Of peace. Kairo approached the tree slowly and ran his hand across its bark.

“You’ve grown,” a voice said.

He froze. The voice had not come from behind. It had come—from the tree. He took a step back, eyes scanning his surroundings. No one.

“What... Who’s there?” he asked.

“I’ve been waiting, Kairo,” the tree spoke again, its voice low and measured. “You carry something older than you know. Something that once belonged to a man named Velran.”

Kairo’s chest tightened. “The locket?”

“Yes,” the tree replied. “Velran was a scientist. A visionary. A man who dared to search beyond the limits of this world.”

“A scientist?” Kairo asked. “Why would a scientist have anything to do with this… scythe… or me?”

The branches shifted, though there was no wind. “Because he discovered the locket first. Buried deep beneath the earth, in a cavern long forgotten. He studied it. Obsessed over it. And in time, it revealed to him five ancient paths—what he called the Five Doctrines.”

Kairo’s eyes widened. “He unlocked all of them?”

“No,” the tree answered. “Only three. He spent his entire life mastering them. But before he could reach the fourth, the visions began. Visions of a figure cloaked in darkness… and of Earth’s annihilation. He uncovered a prophecy,” the tree went on. “One that foretold Earth’s destruction. A man would rise. A man with no name. No origin. He would appear exactly one hundred years after Velran’s discovery. And he would bring ruin.”

Kairo’s heart pounded. “Velran tried to warn the world,” the voice said, now tinged with sadness. “But no one listened. He was mocked. Branded insane. Alone, he prepared to stop the man. But he failed.”

“What happened to him?” Kairo asked.

“One year before the prophecy’s end, the man appeared. And Velran—despite his strength, despite his knowledge—was defeated. He had not mastered all Five Doctrines. It wasn’t enough.”

Kairo swallowed hard. The wind carried a strange stillness now. “Then why me?” he whispered. “Why would the locket choose me?”

“Because you are the last seed,” the tree said. “The last chance. A legacy. A hope. And it found you.”

Kairo lowered his gaze. “I didn’t ask for this.”

“No one ever does,” the tree answered. “But time is short. The prophecy is almost complete. You have just more 3 years. three year to master the Five Doctrines. three year to face the man Velran could not defeat.”

Silence. Then Kairo raised his head. His voice, though uncertain, was steady. “What are the Five Doctrines?”

The tree’s branches slowly extended eastward—toward the rising sun. “That,” it said, “is where your journey begins.GO in that direction”

And suddenly—Kairo woke up. His breath was heavy as his eyes locked onto the cracked ceiling above him. The room was silent. Too silent. Then he noticed it.

The locket lay on the floor, right where he had thrown it. He looked down. He was still wearing the jacket. It hadn’t been a dream.
